Salt features

May 2025

At itris, I introduced a term internally to help guide our approach to design which was "salt features".

It was a way to describe features that, once added would be difficult or impossible to remove without disruption to users. Like salt in cooking, if you add too much, it ruins a dish. The best way is to season little by little and keep tasting along the way until you get to your perfectly seasoned Michelin star feature (or product). You can always add salt but you can't take it away.

A practical example from my experience illustrates this clearly. While designing a new Business Terms feature at itris, we considered adding settings that could prevent users from creating jobs for companies without agreed terms. Initially, it seemed logical to include this as a configurable setting tied to Business Terms right away. However, we had long-term plans for a broader company compliance initiative that could be a more appropriate home for this functionality. Adding these settings to Business Terms prematurely would mean future friction both for users adapting to a shift in workflow, and for our team, as we'd later need to unwind decisions already embedded into the product.

We chose to hold off on including these settings initially. Although this meant a temporary trade-off in immediate functionality, we avoided unnecessary future work. This approach embodied the careful, intentional thinking behind salt features.

This became a sort of mantra that we would draw upon to help us think clearly about a project's scope, especially in ambiguous projects. It's tempting to include everything up-front, but decisions made in haste can solidify into long-term problems. Even minor-seeming choices, like adding a column to a table or a user preference, might deserve thoughtful consideration if there was a chance we would revisit or remove it later.

A good example of how impactful these commitments can be is when Apple changed the position of the Safari tab bar on iOS from the top to the bottom. The intention was to improve reachability but users were already accustomed to the tab bar being at the top, and the change disrupted their mental model. Even though objectively, this made much more sense, it wasn’t about whether the new position was better; it was that the existing pattern had become deeply ingrained, making the switch jarring and uncomfortable. Even smart, well-intended changes meet resistance when they break what users have already internalised. That’s why the concept of salt features was important, because premature permanence can limit your future manoeuvrability.

In fast moving product teams, throughput is often considered a measure of success and overall it is. But focusing on short-term momentum without careful consideration introduces significant long-term risks. Salt features epitomise decisions that boost short-term progress but quietly accumulate design and technical debt.

Ultimately, taking some time to consider decisions, no matter how minor they initially seem, can proactively manage complexity and maintain product flexibility over time. Like cooking, intentional design seasoning enhances the overall experience. But add too much salt too quickly, and you might have no choice but to throw it away and start again.
