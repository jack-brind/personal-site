TL;DR

Most design case studies are polished success stories. This isn't one of them. It's about an 18-month redesign of itris, a recruitment CRM I worked on — a project I poured everything into, only for it to be cancelled before launch. But this story isn't about the product that shipped. It's about growth, hindsight, and the lessons I learned along the way.

I fell into design unexpectedly after joining the company as a documentation writer straight out of university. By questioning inconsistencies in the product — like a button that literally did nothing — I was gradually invited to suggest improvements. That curiosity grew into a career. Eventually, I became the company's first product designer and was tasked with redesigning their ageing desktop CRM product, which we rebuilt and released in 2020.

That launch should have been a proud moment, but I felt uneasy. My skills had evolved faster than the product had. The UI no longer reflected my design sensibilities, and we'd built it on a tech stack (WPF) that was already starting to creak under modern expectations. Despite the positive feedback, I knew we could do better — and I wanted another shot at it.

In 2023, I got that chance. The company greenlit a web-based version, and I saw it as a fresh start. This wasn't just a port — it was an opportunity to rethink everything: the architecture, the interactions, the experience. I brought a decade of hard-won lessons, helped choose Vue.js and Storybook, and began laying the foundation for a scalable, modern design system with a small cross-functional team.

This series documents that journey — not just the pixels and patterns, but the thinking behind them. It's not a conventional case study, because it wasn't a conventional project. It's a reflection on what it means to let go, to evolve, and to design not just a better product, but a better version of yourself.

Introduction

In my previous article, I outlined the need for a redesign of itris. This article follows more of a case study like structure, but it's still not your traditional cookie cutter case study - sorry to disappoint. It does, however, cover the strategy and thought process behind tearing down the house and rebuilding.

Like a building, a product needs to have rock solid foundations to support something that will in place for a long time. A product's usability is directly tied to the IA and navigation so getting this right was paramount. Starting with the IA is like creating the container to hold the rest of the redesigned views, so starting here made sense.

Auditing the current IA

I'd love to share a nice clean process here but to tell you the truth, due to it being a side project for much of the time because of other priorities, the process was messy and scattered. I'd have the odd spurt of focus on it and then have to drop it again so momentum was hard to keep up. Therefore, the process wasn't linear at all.

At first, looking at what the redesign could be, it was tempting to jump straight in and tackle the key parts, but it was important to maintain a level of focus and the chrome was arguably the part of the itris 9 that needed the most attention. And I loved it. I really enjoy fiddling with the details on core functional components so it was good fun working on this. I enjoy these types of challenges.

This is what the itris 9 chrome looked like:

I could list more issues than there are pixels in this screen. The key ones are:

  • Colour is not timeless
  • The action bar blue has a poor contrast ratio with the white foregrounds of 3.32:1
  • The nav bar at the top has a load of icons without labels (even internally this was becoming a problem!)
  • The sidebar sub-menu was the same for all main records and a bit redundant
  • The sidebar sub-menu interaction of opening and closing was clunky
  • The action bar was directly related to the loaded view but there was a disconnect
  • The admin bar was in the same hierarchical level as the action buttons for the loaded view
  • The admin bar didn't have labels
  • The nav bar and action bar took up far too much vertical real estate
  • The status bar was unnecessary most of the time and could easily be moved

There's plenty more that I won't bore you with.

Process

Narrative - needed to break everything out (37 things) then piece it all back together in a better way. Dismantling an existing product to piece the puzzle back together again in a better way. Any analogies here? Homeland - Carrie with a pin board. The process was pretty simple. Break it and put it back together in a better way. But that isn't so easy:

I needed to break these things out into their own separate pieces. First, I ignored the action bar - these were directly related to records and had absolutely no right to be part of the IA and chrome.

What I was left with was the nav bar buttons, the quick find, the sidebar menu and admin panel. So I noted down each piece that I needed to find a new home for - a total of 37 items.

At this point, I needed to work out how to group these items logically. It didn't make sense to have these as one long list because it would lack any sort of hierarchy and look too cluttered and lead to sidebar overload.

From customer feedback, we knew that some customers wanted to be able to customise their workflows. For example, for a recruiter, areas like timesheets and financial exports weren't important. Likewise, for compliance officers, they only really needed access to compliance areas and not really anything related to recruiter workflows. Financial people only really needed access to jobs and placements and candidate records. So we knew we wanted to make it customisable.

So I grouped all the items in logical groupings. At this point, the groupings weren't perfect, but they gave me at least some semblance of order.

I then looked at some products that I use regularly and other popular products to get some inspiration for the IA. Almost all of the products that I use had just a single a sidebar. I also looked at different design systems and some popular Figma UI libraries like Untitled UI, for example.

Inspiration was drawn from MacOS, Linear, Campsite, Notion, Confluence, YouTube, etc.

From then, I had a good idea of how I wanted to proceed. So I started looking at options. One of the key things that I wanted to change was the dark blue background for the chrome. It was called Midnight Blue and it was the base colour for itris 9, but it looked old very quickly. For a product to be timeless, or at least look fresher for longer, neutral colours is what I was aiming to use. For the new product, I also wanted to have a dark mode (this was asked for countless times, but there was too much design and technical debt to get it working in itris 9).

Another thing I wanted to add to the sidebar was a nice productivity section that allowed you to pin records, views and areas to the sidebar in a section that would come to be known as Shortcuts. For now, however, this was sidelined until I had the rest of the sidebar figured out.

So I started to put together some ideas for a sidebar - this is just a selection of some of the designs I worked through and I didn't love any of them. I did quite like the style of number 3 with the blue accent and elevated white button with legs coming out but something about it just didn't really sit well with me.

The other thing I needed to consider was how the items in the sidebar would be customised and none of these worked very nicely with that. I experimented with the idea of having a curated list at the top and a section for all items at the bottom where you could pin and unpin to the curated list but this didn't feel right either.

Eventually, after lot of playing around, things started to fall into place, but there were some things I needed to work out first.

Submenu

In the itris 9 sidebar, the main records (applicant, company, contact, job and placement records) had sub-menus when you clicked on them.

Clicking on the main header menu item would expand and collapse the menu, but it also opened the associated Record Hub which is a sort of dashboard for the record where you can your recently opened records, favourites, recently created, and some more. So this menu had 2 contexts:

  1. Opened the hub
  2. Opened / closed the sub-menu

This is a bit awkward with 2 behaviours so I wanted to fix this. In each sub menu, we had the same 3 buttons - New, Search and Group. New would obviously open the new record form which made sense. But search and group would just open the record search and record groups areas with the record type pre-selected. So for example, if you clicked Groups under Companies, it would open the Groups area with Companies pre-selected.

The interaction the sub menu wasn't smooth either. The interaction was janky and it would move the menu around which didn't feel nice either. So I explored what we could do to make this better in the new version.

Sidenote: One of my UX pet hates is where a loaded view refreshes after a period of time and you select an option that changes 0.132ms before you click it and you click the wrong thing. This happens on the Siri suggestions list on iOS and it boils my piss.

The main problem with the interaction was that the sub-menus were mutually exclusive so you couldn't have both open at the same time, nor was there any signifier on the menu to afford opening or any way to stop this behaviour.

The group and search buttons were redundant as they were just nice workflow enhancements that didn't have that much of an impact. Removing these was a happy tradeoff. So that left me with creating new records. In itris 9, this was the primary workflow for creating new records so I wanted to preserve this paradigm to make the transition a painless as possible for users that had already built up that mental model.

I had previously explored the idea of having a curated list of pinned menu items and another section for all tools that and users would be able to move menu items between those lists by pinning and unpinning. The pin button would be in the right side of the menu. However, late explorations led me away from that route but I still liked the idea of having an action on the menu item. So I settled on having a new button on each menu items where a new instance of something could be created and this would be shown on hover. This kept context nicely.

After that I needed to work out how customisation would work. Bit first, I needed to finalise the groupings.

Groupings

We then ranked them high to low priority (based on user's interactions with them). With a total of 37 pieces of the puzzle, I couldn't put all of them in the menu. Some that were infrequently used needed to go elsewhere. These were like the secondary navigations that were needed now and again and so didn't belong in the main groupings.
