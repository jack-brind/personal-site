WRITING March 2024

The Redesign That Never Shipped

I spent 18 months redesigning a platform I'd worked on for over a decade. It was my redemption arc. And then it got cancelled.

Role:
Product Designer
Duration:
18 months
Status:
Cancelled

Preface

Most designers write about success stories — design projects that shipped, metrics that soared, glowing customer feedback. And it makes sense right? Why would you write a case study and pitch yourself to would-be hiring managers without showing them how good you are?

Well, this isn't one of those stories.

This is about a project I spent over 18 months working on — a complete redesign of itris recruitment CRM — that never launched. It was my chance to redeem past design mistakes, apply a decade of experience sharpening my design skills and product sense, and build something I could finally be truly proud of. And then it got cancelled.

This isn't a series of traditional case studies. Not really, anyway, because let's be honest, most cookie cutter case studies are kind of dull. Packed with diagrams of the Double Diamond Methodology and neatly packaged Design Thinking infographics walking us through empathise, define, ideate, prototype, and test. Not to mention Bob, the persona that was made up as your ideal user. Does anyone really read these? I certainly don't. So this is more a story with some case study content sprinkled in. More of a fly on the wall, behind the scenes piece. It's a story of growth, written across multiple articles.

This series is about what I learned — not just about product design, but about letting go of something you'd grown attached to. Why a series, you ask? This project was huge; too long for one article. This first entry sets the scene and context. Think of this series as a tasting menu. And this article? The amuse-bouche.

Where it all started

Many a designer, regardless of domain, will know the feeling of looking back at your earlier work wincing like you've just bitten into a lemon.

Did I really think that was good?

I've been a product designer for over a decade. I landed my first job when I was fresh out of university having picked up a Bachelor's degree in Philosophy. Unlike many of my university friends who had completed accounting and finance degrees or computer science degrees, I couldn't really see myself becoming a Philosopher for a living.

After exactly 9 days of freedom in-between university and real-life (i.e. getting a job), I joined a software company that was looking for someone to work on a fixed-term contract for 12 months re-writing the documentation for their product. To write the documentation, I had to learn the product and I would consistently stumble across things that were inconsistent, awkward, clunky and then some things that just simply didn't work.

I recall one scenario where I clicked on a button in a settings screen and nothing happened. I asked the lead developer to look into it and let me know what it is supposed to do.

Developer
Ah yeah, you've found a Comedy Button.
A comedy button?
Developer
Yeah, they're buttons that don't do anything. There's no code behind it.
Ok. So what do we do about this one?
Developer
Why don't you have a think about what we should do and come back to me…

And there it was — my first baby step into product design. Over the course of the next few months, I was trusted to come back with more suggestions for improvements that formed part of a short initiative to improve parts of the UI. The improvements were a drop in the ocean, but it lit a spark I didn't expect — I really enjoyed it.

Oh, and that comedy button was removed, by the way.

A brief history of itris Recruitment CRM

The product was called itris — a Recruitment CRM, established in 1998. Built to be the operational backbone of recruitment agencies, at its core, it combines the functionality of a sales tool, Applicant Tracking System (ATS), and Customer Relationship Management (CRM) solution into one platform.

Speed and accuracy is everything in recruitment. The faster a recruiter can respond to a job or match a suitable candidate, the more likely they are to succeed. That need for speed shaped a lot of the product's early design decisions, as did the fundamental focus on accuracy.

When I joined, the flagship version was itris 8. It was released in 2008 and the design was heavily influenced by Windows Vista with a lot of skeuomorphic glossy buttons and beveled edges. It was built in Delphi (which, I'm told, was pretty popular back in the day), using a SQL database for data storage — very cutting edge if you're into early-2000s throwbacks.

But it was showing its age, and the market was moving on. Competitors were gaining ground, and a big decision needed to be made — one the CEO would later call "one of his biggest regrets." (We'll get to that).

Enter itris 9

Around the time I joined, there were murmurs of re-writing the entire product from the ground up using a more modern stack - C# and WPF and there had been some background work going on behind the scenes to accommodate this new product. Towards the end of 2014, when the articles had been largely re-written, and after having shown my value, I was presented with the opportunity of staying on and being the company's first dedicated designer.

My brief? Design the future of itris.

It's worth noting that my experience of product design thus far was mostly question like "Should we make these icons a bit bigger?" and "Is it worth adding a little description to this section?". Equipped with exactly zero product sense, Microsoft Paint, and a worrying amount of confidence, I gave it my best shot. Looking back, I quite literally had no idea what I was doing. It took me at least a year to even discover that UX design was a thing — I was too busy designing things that looked cool after seeing things on Dribbble.

But over time, through a mix of time and a lot of trial and error, I began to figure things out and it would lead to me redesigning an entire platform from scratch. A platform that was complex and already very mature serving customers around the world.

By 2016, itris 9 had began full time development and the initial designs that I had put together were starting to be implemented in code. At that early stage, I was still finding my feet and the design was influenced by the flat design trend, popularised by iOS 7 and Windows Metro. From that point, the design had been established because, well, I couldn't feasibly keep changing it - I had to settle on something.

At first, the "design team" was just me and our Training Manager, who had years of customer insight. Then, in 2017, a new PM came onboard and introduced radical ideas like MVP, kanban boards, feature creep, and (brace yourself) Jira.

It took 3 more challenging years to finally release the new product in 2020, right in the middle of a global pandemic. Perfect timing.

That feeling should have been one of jubilation — celebration and pride in the achievement. We finally had a product that was rebuilt from scratch and a complete redesign. But for me, it was more of a strange, melancholic anti-climax. I think it was because my taste and design sensibilities had evolved faster than itris had.

The product itself was solid—robust in terms of workflow—but the UI just looked… amateurish. It was too cluttered, lacked hierarchy, had poor contrast. Basically, despite my own hubris, I knew I could do better.

Everyone else loved it. Internally, it was seen as a huge upgrade over itris 8. It was also really well received by customers. But I felt stuck designing in a style I no longer believed in.

That's a weird place to be: when your skills and taste improve after the work is done, and you can see exactly what you'd do differently but you can't change it. Sure, I made some small incremental changes, but at its core, the design language was deeply rooted in the product and changing it fundamentally was just not commercially viable.

At this point in my journey, I had 6 or so years of experience and I had learned a lot. I'd gone from Microsoft Paint to Photoshop to Adobe Xd and amassed a tonne of knowledge and become the champion of design in the business. I desperately wanted to redesign it again! For the next few years I had to design in a style that I didn't like that much. It was frustrating.

Still, the product worked well at launch, and customers really liked it, and they still do today. But as features grew and the user base expanded, more explicit limitations began to surface beyond the aesthetics. Interfaces became cluttered and workflows became over-complicated as more features and improvements were added. Some parts just simply didn't scale. More and more customer requests ended up with us saying 'hmm, we probably need to change these x things before we can facilitate that' - we were chasing out tail. We had accrued a lot of design and technical debt already, faster than we could pay it back.

Where things started to break

To make matters more complicated, itris had always been a desktop product, not accessible inside a browser (which was a hard sell in the post Covid hybrid/remote working era) which is increasingly what customers and prospects wanted. Remember I mentioned the CEO's biggest regret? This was it - choosing to do another desktop product instead of a web product. We had spent years rebuilding the product in a desktop environment, which, at the time was still relevant (just about) but over time, it became clear that if we wanted to stay relevant, we needed to create a web front end.

Aside from the fact that it was what customers and prospects wanted, there were technical constraints and challenges that had plagued from day one. For example:

  • WPF had no native responsiveness like @media queries
  • Animations were almost impossible due to WPF's single UI thread
  • WPF was nowhere near as rich as HTML & CSS
  • There was no emoji support
  • Customers and prospects repeatedly asked for @metion functionality, but it wouldn't have been easy
  • Dark mode needed to be manually implemented but there was too much technical debt
  • No native SVG support

There's plenty more that I won't bore you with.

A chance for redemption

Due to increasing demands, in 2023, the CEO made the decision to sanction a web client for itris. The backend was solid so this would be a new client layer, giving users the flexibility to work in either the desktop or web version. It would be less work than before because we were only creating a web view on top. We had some modifications to make in the backend to support it but compared to before, it wouldn't be as big.

And for me, it was a perfect scenario: a second chance. Not just to "port" the product to the web, but to completely reimagine its foundations — the architecture, navigation, interactions, and overall experience. This time, I brought a decade of hard-won lessons, sharper instincts, and a better eye for what truly matters in product design. We didn't want a surface-level facelift. We wanted to rethink how itris worked, felt, and fit into people's daily lives. It was a chance to build something better — and, quietly, to prove to myself how far I'd come.

It was a chance to revisit my own thinking, correct my earlier mistakes, and approach it all with fresh eyes and a (slightly more) grown-up perspective. I'd learned how to make better decisions, ask better questions, and build systems that scale. A chance to simplify, streamline, and finally design the product I wish I'd known how to make the first time.

We assembled a small team of 3 - me (design), a PM and an engineer to think about the initial scope. We also involved the whole engineering team every Thursday for 6 weeks to throw our heads together and discuss the path forwards. Ultimately, we settled on using Vue.js for the front end framework and I also suggested that we use Storybook for the components. I would also build a design system to help speed everything up and introduce some consistency (I will write about this in an upcoming post).

Huge redesigns for established products are a huge risk, but sometimes necessary. You need to make changes to facilitate growth, but at the same time, a dramatic change could end up alienating users that are comfortable. However, we had a somewhat unique position in that itris 9 was a desktop product and so this web version would be separate. Users would be able to run both versions side by side so they could ease into it and we'd be able to get feedback, similar to when Figma went from UI2 to UI3.

There is a fantastic post by Linear on redesigns that I would urge you to read.

The plan

Looking back, the hardest part wasn't choosing frameworks or picking colours, it was carving out time and space to rethink the foundations of the product itself. With just me on design and ongoing work on itris 9, progress was slow and sporadic. But gradually, we started to zoom out and make sense of what needed to come first.

We kicked off "Web Fridays" which were dedicated sessions where the PM and I casual sessions where the PM and I would talk about ideas, untangle parts of the IA, and sketch early flows. Those conversations made it clear that before we could design anything, we had to get the structure right and approach the redesign systematically.

For the next 18 months, we would be making good headway on the project and everything was looking great.

And then everything changed...

In 2024, the business was acquired. Not long after, the new owners made a strategic decision to pull the plug on the web redesign entirely — choosing instead to re-skin another product they already owned.

Just like that, the project I'd spent over 18 months researching, planning, sketching, prototyping, and nurturing… was gone.

No big drama. No fanfare. Just a quiet full stop.

What I Took With Me

Was it disappointing? Of course. I'd poured so much of myself into that project — not just my time, but my taste, my experience, my growth as a designer. And to see it shelved for business reasons beyond my control was, frankly, gutting.

But I don't regret a minute of it.

That web redesign taught me more than many projects that did ship. It sharpened my instincts, my systems thinking, my product sense. It gave me the chance to redeem earlier design choices and think more holistically. It reminded me how to fight for the user — even when the outcome isn't guaranteed.

And most importantly, it helped me grow.

That growth doesn't go away just because the work didn't launch. I still carry it with me — in how I think, how I work, and how I approach every challenge that comes next.
